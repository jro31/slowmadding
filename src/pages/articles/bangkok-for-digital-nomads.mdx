Thailand's capital is the hub of South-East Asia. Bangkok has extensive transport links to all of Thailand and beyond. Yet remaining within this mammoth city there is so much variety spread over its many districts that you can find almost anything you're looking for without ever leaving, so long as you know where to look.

Accommodation starts from dirt-cheap, and there is a huge offering of social opportunities, with Facebook groups and Meetups aplenty, as well as being one of the funnest places I've ever been to go out alone. There is every kind of bar you can imagine and many you can't, and Bangkok can cater to almost any culinary taste, including being one of the most vegan-friendly cities on earth. Vitally for a digital nomad it has some of the fastest Internet in the world, including mobile networks, so you never have to worry about staying connected.

Green space is a little limited in Bangkok, so choose your accommodation wisely to be sure you're within each reach of one of the city's parks. As in any major city there's plenty of traffic (and plenty of pollution). Buses stretch to all corners of the city, although knowing which one to take is less straight forward. More commonly used by first-timers, the ever-expanding skytrain (BTS) and underground (MRT) networks are a quick and affordable way of getting around, and taxis can be hailed almost anywhere. Most are honest unless you look like a very gullible tourist.

## Which area to stay in Bangkok

Bangkok centres around Sukhumvit. Other people will dispute that, claiming that Siam or even Silom are the centre of Bangkok, and that's ok. They're allowed to be wrong. When coming to Bangkok for the first time and trying to figure-out where to stay, start at Sukhumvit (around the <ExternalLink url='https://maps.app.goo.gl/Xg26jaXChYttBn7NA'>Terminal 21 shopping mall</ExternalLink>) and work outwards from there until you find what you're looking for.

If you head north-west just a few hundred metres, you'll get to <ExternalLink url='https://maps.app.goo.gl/oAMsdqDJrtFT9qYt9'>Nana</ExternalLink>, which is a disgustingly touristy area often favoured by western retirees. If you're coming to Bangkok for daytime drinking and prostitutes, then look no further.

I don't recommend it as an area to stay. If you really want to come here to party then it's easy to get to, but it's not a pleasant area to stay in.

Continue north-west from there another kilometre and you'll make it to <ExternalLink url='https://maps.app.goo.gl/iaXMD9CX4nwAMjQa7'>Siam</ExternalLink>. If you love shopping, and particularly shopping malls, then this is your dream. Siam Paragon and Central World are two of best known hi-so shopping malls in the area, and MBK is great for a faux Thai experience. There's not much else here of interest if I'm honest, and as the shopping malls are so easily accessible, I'd recommend staying elsewhere, and just coming to Siam when you want to go shopping.

Continue on and you'll get to <ExternalLink url='https://maps.app.goo.gl/Jbnu3ccEQ97PrZMA7'>Khao San Road</ExternalLink> and <ExternalLink url='https://maps.app.goo.gl/jQcpibjnFuCZ1rjp9'>China Town</ExternalLink>. Khao San is famously the place where backpackers stay in Bangkok. There are some very cheap room on and around Khao San Road, and it's a renowned party spot. By this far out, the transport links are a little lacking, but it's still easy to get a taxi to Khao San if you want a night out. If you're a digital nomad who actually has work to get done then it's not a good area to stay in. It's much for the younger unemployed crowd.

Chinatown is very cheap. If you're here on a budget then it's a lively place where you can find a cheap room. Otherwise I'd recommend avoiding it.

Starting again from Terminal 21, if instead you go south-east, the further you travel the more you'll be in areas favoured by expats over tourists. Firstly you'll get in <ExternalLink url='https://maps.app.goo.gl/zqUjKRKbRip3TsC29'>Phrom Pong</ExternalLink>. I recommend this area, particularly to someone coming to Bangkok for the first time. You're away from the extreme tourism of Nana and Sukhumvit, but you're still in a very forigner-friendly area. The entire city is easily accessible from here as you're walking distance from Terminal 21 and a couple of Bangkok's parks.

Beyond Phrom Pong you get to <ExternalLink url='https://maps.app.goo.gl/f5u5rtgS2zriWnpf8'>Thong Lo</ExternalLink>, and then to <ExternalLink url='https://maps.app.goo.gl/ejcsQQqsjzqguJkZ9'>Ekkamai</ExternalLink>. Both areas are heavily favoured by expats so are great if you want to meet foreigners living rather than visiting Bangkok. They're more 'trendy' areas, so you can find some overpriced bars if you're into that kind of thing. Travelling on from there things get more local and less expensive. <ExternalLink url='https://maps.app.goo.gl/QwRyuwjg2oucFwLb7'>On Nut</ExternalLink> is an area popular with expats on a tighter budget.

Going south-west from Terminal 21 you get to Sathorn/Silom area. <ExternalLink url='https://maps.app.goo.gl/qP9FNoXyDchk8eARA'>Silom</ExternalLink> was once a bustling area of the city, but since the pandemic it's died a bit. I love <ExternalLink url='https://maps.app.goo.gl/TkXqKsRfuUrsYB8V7'>Sathorn</ExternalLink>. It's a deceptively big district that I lived in for more than three years, and while some areas are popular with expats, it has some very local parts. It's not a cheap area to stay in if you're on a tight budget.

Heading north from Sukhumvit, you'll get to areas of the city that are visited much less by tourists, but that still feel like the city centre. <ExternalLink url='https://maps.app.goo.gl/abDyKZXZmhUUmVtU6'>Rama 9</ExternalLink> has some more (much better value) shopping malls but there isn't too much more there. I lived and worked in Ladprao for a couple of years, and in a way it feels like its own city, having its own downtown area around <ExternalLink url='https://maps.app.goo.gl/5dNb8DBAAbEswx3KA'>Phahon Yothin</ExternalLink>. There are a couple of shopping malls there and Chattuchak has three inter-connected parks which make the biggest green space within the city. And while Chattuchak is famed for its weekend market, this isn't an area that tourists usually choose to stay in, so there aren't too many hotels or foreigner-aimed businesses away from the market.

For years I wanted to live between Saphan Kwai and <ExternalLink url='https://maps.app.goo.gl/8pMTwj1YfdLVLSgE7'>Ari</ExternalLink>. I always thought it was the perfect area, bustling with life and only a short walk from Chattuchak Park, but away from all the tourists and within easy reach of some fun bars around Victory Monument and Ladprao. Unfortunately I never managed to live there and it's so void of tourists that I've never been able to find anywhere there that I actually want to stay as a digital nomad. If you're looking to stay in a good, local area with lots to do nearby, then this is an area to consider. If you can find a room.

If you like staying in big cities, then Bangkok has exactly what you're looking for. The trouble is finding it, especially if you've never been here. Touristy areas can be easily visited for the day, but I don't recommend staying in them. If you've never been here before and you're completely unsure where to go, then around Phrom Pong/Thong Lo is a safe option. You'll have everything you need nearby and most people you encounter will speak English.

Bonita is what I think of as the OG vegan restaurant in Bangkok. It only opened in 2012, so I'm sure it wasn't the first, but it was the first that I went to, and back then there really wasn't much else vegan-friendly in this city.

Because of this nostalgia I perhaps view Bonita with rose-tinted spectacles, but even now I recommend it as one of the best vegan restaurants here and should definitely be on your list of places to eat at when in Bangkok.

## Where to run in Bangkok

Bangkok has a couple of really good places to run; other than that it's pretty dire. Although you'll see people who do, the amount of congestion of both pedestrians and cars make running along the street an absolute no-go for me.

Many areas are congested with people, the pavements are littered with motorcycles, the surface is really uneven, there are so many cars that the pollution makes it unpleasant, and those same cars make crossing the road a pain. I would recommend avoiding running anywhere but in the local parks.

I lived in Ladprao back in the pre-smartphone age (or at least before I got my first smartphone in 2014). That matters, because up in that area of the city you have three closely connected parks. Chatuchak Park is the best known of these, but it is connected to Queen Sirikit Park and Wachirabenchathat Park. If you're in that area, this is a great place to run, but the consequence of me not owning a smartphone when I lived there, is I don't have any Strava maps I can share of my routes.

As a new tourist in Bangkok, it's unlikely that you'll be staying in that area, so let me instead focus on a couple of options that you have in the more popular areas for foreign visitors.

The first of these is pretty simple: Benchasiri Park. It's a small park next to Emporium shopping mall and to Phrom Pong BTS station. It's roughly 700m around the outside, so for a 5k run it's seven laps. Doing any more than that becomes pretty cumbersome, and it can get busy in the mornings and evenings with a couple of annoying bottlenecks on the route. However, if you come here in the daytime it's quiet enough that you can have a pleasant run. There are toilets in the park and a couple of vendors, so if you're adjusting to the heat in Bangkok, this is a good run to start with as you'll have emergency snacks and drinks nearby.

The rest of the runs I'm going to detail will build into my favourite Bangkok route, but it's a little hard to explain to someone who's never been here, so I'll go step-by-step. Looking at the Strava maps below will make it clearer.

The most obvious place to run in Bangkok is Lumphini Park. I spent three years living in Sathorn between 2015 and 2018 for the sole reason that I wanted to be as close to Lumphini Park as possible, because it's such a great place to run. I've really got my laps in there. Although you can make your own route through the inside of the park if you want to, there's a very established running lap that goes around the outside of the park, and is just slightly over 2.5km. It's a wide track the whole way around, so even in the mornings and evenings when it's a lot busier, you still have the space to go at a good pace if you fancy it (Thai people run notoriously slowly, so this is important). As always, I recommend running in the daytime, but that's because my sadomasochistic side loves the heat. At that time you'll have few-to-no other runners to worry about, although you will likely see a lot of cats and a lot of water monitor lizards. It's worth noting that the running track doubles as a cycling track between 10am and 3pm (cycling is not allowed in Lumphini outside of these hours), so don't make any sharp turns without looking behind you if you come at that time.

Closer to Sukhumvit you'll find Benchakitti Park. I use the term 'park' slightly loosely, because it's more a lake with a track around it. The track that you'll likely run on is shared with pedstrians. Despite clear markings telling the pedestrians to be on one side of it, while runners should be on the other, they'll largely ignore this, so all the more reason to come at a quiet time. There's another track, separated from this one for cyclists, although you can also run/walk along that if you fancy. I'd recommend the main track though. It's wider, smoother, and doesn't have any bikes on it. At roughly 1.8km, each lap of the lake is just over a mile.

When I last lived here, that was the extent of Benchakitti Park, but it's since expanded to be roughly three times the size, with the addition of the connected Benchakitti Forest Park, which fully opened in 2022. In the south half of the forest park, there's a road that's suitable for runners, and above the forest park is the Benchakitti Forest Park Skywalk, which is long bridge that goes diagonally across almost the entire forest park and is good for running along. You can try running through the rest of the forest park, but it's very zig-zaggy and hard to see where you're going, and I've seen park security tell runners to stick to the main running road before (although never to me).

That's all well and good, but wouldn't it be wonderful if you could run between Lumphini Park and Benchakitti Park without having to cross any roads?

Well surprise, you can.

A little known feature of Bangkok, is the raised walkway coloquially known as 'The Green Mile'. I used to run along it back when I lived in Bangkok. At that time it connected Lumphini Park to Benchakitti Park in a way that you really had to know it was there in order to find it. With the addition of Benchakitti Forest Park, it's a little easier to find because the Benchakitti Forest Park Skywalk joins The Green Mile about halfway along.

The Green Mile is basically a long walkway open only to pedestrians and cyclists that connects the north-west corner of Benchakitti Park to Thanon Witthayu (Wireless Road), a very short distance from Lumphini Park. So you can connect these three parks without having to cross a single road, or worry about a single car or motorcycle.

The typical route I do, is to start at the south end of Benchakitti Park, do a half-lap before going onto The Green Mile, running to and then doing a lap of Lumphini, before going back onto The Green Mile, turning-off onto the Benchakitti Forest Park Skywalk, running down that to the end, and then finishing back at the south end of Benchakitti Park. That route is roughly 7.7km, but you can obviously add-on as you need by doing additional laps of any of the three parks that it takes you through.

It's not really a route to do for speed, largely because The Green Mile includes some staircases that'll distort your pace on Strava, but other than that it's a lovely route to run, and my favourite route to do in Bangkok.

There are a few other small parks or running tracks in Bangkok that you can use for running in a pinch. If you want to have a good run here though, while still being somewhat central, I'd recommend staying around one of the parks that I've mentioned. Having them within walking distance really does make Bangkok enjoyable.

## Being sociable in Bangkok

There's probably no city in the world that I've found it easier to be sociable in than Bangkok. The Meetup community in Bangkok is really, really active, so you can find an event that you want to attend pretty much seven days per week. Whether that be running clubs, meeting people over coffee, doing yoga, language exchange or going out and getting shit-faced, you're going to be able to find plenty of Meetups to suit your taste, and for whatever reason, I found that the people I met at Meetups in Bangkok were more open to staying in touch and being subsequently social than in other places in the world.

While I obviously suggest finding events to suit your taste, groups that I've attended and can vouch for are the '<ExternalLink url='https://www.meetup.com/lumpini-running-club/'>Lumpini Running Club Bangkok</ExternalLink>', which runs in Lumphini Park twice per week on Tuesdays and Thursdays in the early evening, often followed by dinner. The '<ExternalLink url='https://www.meetup.com/bangkok-cultural-exchange-meetup/'>Bangkok Cultural Exchange Meetup</ExternalLink>' runs language exchange events that actually are about exchanging language. I say that with surprise, because most language exchange events are just a guise for everyone to get drunk while speaking English. A language exchange group that is absolutely nothing to do with exchanging language is '<ExternalLink url='https://www.meetup.com/language-exchange-and-activities-and-social-in-bangkok/'>Language Exchange and Activities and Social in Bangkok</ExternalLink>'. They run weekly events at a bar called 'The Hangover', so that should tell you something. If you're interested in getting vegan food while meeting new people, checkout '<ExternalLink url='https://www.meetup.com/bangkok-vegan-meetup/'>Bangkok Vegan Meetup</ExternalLink>', which hosts events at differing vegan-friendly restaurants around Bangkok. There are many other Meetups that I've been to in Bangkok, and I haven't been to a bad one yet, so you'll be able to find something to suit your tastes, no matter what they are.

If for some reason Meetup isn't able to give you what you need, other platforms are similarly active, and Bangkok isn't a hard place to meet people by just going out, given how friendly the locals are and how many tourists there are here.
